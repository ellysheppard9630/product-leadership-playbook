# Technical Discovery

Not every initiative begins with a customer request.

Many of the highest-impact investments in a software platform originate from Engineering.

Examples include:

- Platform modernization
- Cloud migration
- Performance optimization
- Security improvements
- Reliability initiatives
- Technical debt reduction
- Framework upgrades
- Architecture improvements

In these situations, Engineering leads discovery while Product provides business context, prioritization, and organizational alignment.

The objective remains the same:

Reduce uncertainty before committing to implementation.

---

# When Technical Discovery Is Appropriate

Engineering should lead discovery when the greatest uncertainty is technical rather than business-related.

Typical examples include:

- Scaling limitations
- Performance bottlenecks
- Infrastructure improvements
- Security vulnerabilities
- Legacy system replacement
- Framework upgrades
- Data architecture
- Platform reliability

These initiatives often have little customer-facing functionality but create significant long-term business value.

---

# The Goal of Technical Discovery

Technical discovery answers questions such as:

- What is the underlying technical problem?
- Why does it exist?
- What risks does it create?
- What systems are affected?
- What implementation options exist?
- What are the tradeoffs?
- What level of effort is required?
- What happens if we do nothing?

The objective is to create enough understanding for Product and leadership to make informed investment decisions.

---

# Engineering Responsibilities

Engineering leads the investigation.

Typical activities include:

- Root cause analysis
- Architecture reviews
- Performance profiling
- Technical spikes
- Proof of concepts
- Dependency analysis
- Risk identification
- Cost estimation
- Solution design

Engineering should recommend multiple approaches whenever practical.

Each option should clearly communicate:

- Benefits
- Risks
- Complexity
- Estimated effort
- Long-term impact

---

# Product Responsibilities

Product plays a different—but equally important—role during technical discovery.

Product should focus on:

- Understanding business impact
- Prioritizing the work
- Communicating value to stakeholders
- Evaluating customer impact
- Balancing technical investment with roadmap commitments
- Defining success criteria

Product does not need to design the technical solution.

Instead, Product ensures the organization understands why the investment matters.

---

# Discovery Outputs

Technical discovery should end with a recommendation rather than simply describing a problem.

Typical outputs include:

- Problem statement
- Root cause summary
- Current architecture
- Proposed architecture
- Solution options
- Risk assessment
- Estimated effort
- Dependencies
- Business impact
- Recommendation

The recommendation should help leadership decide whether to invest—not simply explain how the technology works.

---

# Evaluating Technical Investments

Not all technical work creates the same value.

Consider factors such as:

## Customer Impact

Will customers notice an improvement?

Examples include:

- Faster response times
- Improved reliability
- Fewer outages
- Better scalability

---

## Business Impact

Does the initiative:

- Reduce operational costs?
- Improve maintainability?
- Reduce support effort?
- Improve delivery speed?
- Lower operational risk?

---

## Strategic Alignment

Does the work support broader organizational goals?

Examples include:

- Cloud migration
- Platform modernization
- AI enablement
- Security initiatives
- Compliance requirements

---

## Cost of Delay

What happens if the work is postponed?

Questions to ask:

- Will future projects become more expensive?
- Does technical debt continue to grow?
- Is platform stability at risk?
- Will customer experience degrade?

Sometimes delaying technical work is more expensive than completing it.

---

# Technical Discovery Is Collaborative

Although Engineering leads technical discovery, Product should remain engaged throughout the process.

Product helps translate technical findings into business language.

Engineering helps Product understand technical tradeoffs.

Neither discipline succeeds in isolation.

---

# Common Mistakes

## Waiting Until Delivery to Raise Technical Concerns

Discovery exists to identify technical risks before implementation begins.

---

## Treating Technical Debt as Invisible Work

Technical debt competes for engineering capacity just like customer features.

Ignoring it increases future delivery costs.

---

## Presenting Only One Solution

Whenever practical, discovery should explore multiple approaches.

Leadership makes better decisions when tradeoffs are visible.

---

## Speaking Only in Technical Terms

Architecture matters.

Business outcomes matter more.

Technical recommendations should explain:

- Why the work matters.
- What risks it reduces.
- What value it creates.

---

# Success Looks Like

Technical discovery is successful when:

- The technical problem is understood.
- Root causes have been identified.
- Risks are known.
- Solution options have been evaluated.
- Leadership understands the business value.
- Engineering can confidently begin implementation.

The objective is not to eliminate uncertainty.

The objective is to reduce uncertainty enough to make confident decisions.

Good technical discovery gives Engineering the autonomy to execute while giving Product the information needed to prioritize wisely.
