# Customer Discovery

Customer discovery is the process of understanding customer problems before proposing solutions.

The objective isn't to validate an idea.

The objective is to understand the customer's world well enough to determine whether a problem is worth solving—and if so, why.

Good discovery often changes the original request.

Sometimes it eliminates the need to build anything at all.

---

# Start with the Problem

Customers usually describe solutions.

Examples include:

- "We need another report."
- "Can you add a checkbox?"
- "We need an API."

Instead of immediately discussing implementation, explore the underlying problem.

Ask questions like:

- What are you trying to accomplish?
- What's difficult today?
- How are you solving this now?
- What happens when this problem occurs?
- How often does it happen?
- Who is affected?

The goal is to understand the job the customer is trying to complete—not simply the feature they requested.

---

# Understand the Impact

Not every problem deserves the same investment.

Evaluate the impact by understanding:

## Frequency

- How often does the problem occur?
- Every day?
- Weekly?
- Monthly?
- Rarely?

---

## Severity

- Is this an inconvenience?
- Does it block work?
- Does it create financial risk?
- Does it affect compliance?
- Does it impact customer satisfaction?

---

## Scope

Determine who experiences the problem.

Examples:

- One user
- One department
- One customer
- Multiple customers
- Every customer

The broader the impact, the greater the potential value.

---

# Separate Symptoms from Root Causes

Customers often describe symptoms.

Discovery should uncover root causes.

Example:

Customer says:

> "Searching takes too long."

Possible root causes:

- Poor search performance
- Missing filters
- Incorrect indexing
- Users searching the wrong data
- Incomplete training

Different root causes require different solutions.

---

# Validate Before Building

Whenever possible, validate assumptions before committing to development.

Examples include:

- Customer interviews
- Product demonstrations
- Usage analytics
- Support case analysis
- Surveys
- Prototype feedback
- A/B testing
- Observation sessions

Validation reduces the risk of building the wrong solution.

---

# Questions Worth Asking

## About the Problem

- What are you trying to accomplish?
- Why is this important?
- How are you solving it today?
- What's frustrating about the current process?
- What happens if nothing changes?

---

## About the Users

- Who experiences this problem?
- How often?
- Who benefits from a solution?
- Who might be negatively impacted?

---

## About Success

- What would success look like?
- How would you know this problem has been solved?
- What would improve?
- What metrics matter?

---

## About Priority

- Is this your biggest challenge today?
- What other initiatives compete for attention?
- Why now?

These questions often reveal whether the request aligns with broader business priorities.

---

# Discovery Artifacts

Customer discovery doesn't require lengthy documentation.

Capture only what the team needs to make informed decisions.

Typical outputs include:

- Problem statement
- Customer personas
- Workflow diagrams
- Journey maps
- Notes from customer conversations
- Key assumptions
- Risks
- Open questions
- Success metrics

The value comes from the conversations—not the documents.

---

# Common Mistakes

## Jumping to Solutions

Avoid discussing implementation before understanding the problem.

---

## Talking More Than Listening

Discovery is primarily about asking thoughtful questions.

Customers should spend more time talking than Product.

---

## Interviewing Only One Customer

One customer's experience may not represent the broader market.

Look for patterns across multiple conversations.

---

## Leading the Customer

Instead of asking:

> "Wouldn't it be helpful if...?"

Ask:

> "Tell me how you currently handle this."

Neutral questions produce more reliable insights.

---

## Assuming Every Request Deserves a Roadmap Spot

Discovery should be willing to conclude:

- Not now.
- Not this way.
- Not enough value.
- Already solved elsewhere.

Saying no is a successful outcome when supported by evidence.

---

# Signs of Successful Discovery

Customer discovery is successful when:

- The problem is clearly understood.
- The affected users are identified.
- Business value is defined.
- Success can be measured.
- Assumptions have been challenged.
- The team understands why the work matters.

At that point, Product can confidently recommend whether the initiative should move forward—or not.

The goal isn't to collect feature requests.

The goal is to make better product decisions.
