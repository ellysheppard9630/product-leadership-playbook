# Prioritization

Every Product organization has more ideas than capacity.

The challenge isn't finding work to do.

The challenge is deciding what not to do.

Prioritization is the discipline of allocating limited time, people, and investment toward the initiatives that create the greatest value for customers and the business.

Saying "yes" to one initiative means saying "no"—or at least "not yet"—to another.

---

# The Purpose of Prioritization

Effective prioritization helps organizations:

- Deliver the highest-value work first
- Align investments with strategy
- Balance customer and technical needs
- Create transparency
- Reduce context switching
- Improve predictability

Prioritization is not about satisfying every stakeholder.

It is about making intentional tradeoffs.

---

# Start With Strategy

Before comparing initiatives, understand what the organization is trying to accomplish.

Questions to ask include:

- What are our strategic goals?
- What outcomes matter most this quarter?
- What investments support those goals?
- What problems are most important to solve?

Without strategy, prioritization becomes reactive.

---

# Prioritize Problems, Not Requests

Customers request solutions.

Product prioritizes problems.

For example:

Customer Request:

> "We need another report."

Underlying Problem:

> "Managers cannot quickly identify pricing discrepancies."

Different solutions may solve the same problem.

Understanding the problem creates more flexibility during discovery.

---

# Factors to Consider

No single factor should determine priority.

Instead, evaluate initiatives across multiple dimensions.

## Customer Value

- How many customers benefit?
- How severe is the problem?
- How frequently does it occur?
- Does it improve customer satisfaction?

---

## Business Value

- Revenue opportunity
- Cost reduction
- Operational efficiency
- Competitive differentiation
- Strategic importance

---

## Technical Value

Some initiatives create value by improving the platform.

Examples include:

- Performance improvements
- Reliability
- Security
- Platform modernization
- Technical debt reduction

These investments may not introduce new features, but they enable future delivery.

---

## Cost of Delay

Consider the consequences of waiting.

Questions to ask:

- Does delaying increase engineering effort?
- Will customers continue experiencing pain?
- Does it create operational risk?
- Does it block future initiatives?

Sometimes delaying work is more expensive than completing it.

---

## Effort

Understanding effort helps Product evaluate opportunity cost.

Effort should inform prioritization—not dictate it.

High-value initiatives often require significant investment.

---

# Portfolio Balance

Healthy Product organizations balance multiple categories of work.

Examples include:

- Customer enhancements
- Strategic initiatives
- Platform modernization
- Technical debt
- Security
- Compliance
- Operational improvements
- Innovation

Over-investing in one category eventually creates problems elsewhere.

---

# Use Discovery Before Prioritization

Ideas should not enter the roadmap without sufficient discovery.

Discovery answers questions such as:

- Is this a real problem?
- How valuable is it?
- What assumptions exist?
- What risks should be considered?

Prioritization becomes significantly easier when discovery is complete.

---

# Prioritization Is Continuous

Priorities should evolve as new information becomes available.

Examples include:

- Customer feedback
- Engineering discoveries
- Market changes
- Business strategy
- Production incidents
- Regulatory requirements

Changing priorities is a sign of learning—not poor planning.

---

# AI and Prioritization

AI can assist Product teams by:

- Summarizing customer feedback
- Identifying recurring themes
- Detecting duplicate requests
- Grouping related initiatives
- Analyzing support trends
- Drafting executive summaries

AI can improve the quality of information.

People remain responsible for prioritization decisions.

---

# Common Mistakes

## Prioritizing the Loudest Voice

The highest-ranking stakeholder does not automatically have the highest-priority request.

Good prioritization relies on evidence rather than influence.

---

## Ignoring Technical Investments

Technical work creates long-term business value.

Organizations that consistently postpone modernization eventually slow their ability to deliver customer value.

---

## Treating Every Request as High Priority

If everything is important, nothing is.

Meaningful prioritization requires difficult decisions.

---

## Prioritizing Without Discovery

Insufficient discovery often leads to incorrect assumptions.

Understand the problem before assigning priority.

---

## Never Revisiting Priorities

Priorities should be reviewed regularly.

What was important six months ago may no longer represent the greatest opportunity.

---

# Questions to Ask

Before prioritizing an initiative, ask:

- What customer problem does this solve?
- What business outcome will it create?
- Does this align with our strategy?
- What happens if we delay?
- What opportunity are we giving up by doing this?
- Have we completed enough discovery?
- Is this the best use of our limited capacity?

---

# Success Looks Like

Successful prioritization creates confidence throughout the organization.

Success means:

- Teams understand why work was selected.
- Leadership understands investment decisions.
- Engineering works on the highest-value initiatives.
- Customers receive meaningful improvements.
- Product can clearly explain every major priority.

The objective of prioritization isn't to build everything.

It's to ensure the organization invests in the work that creates the greatest long-term value.
