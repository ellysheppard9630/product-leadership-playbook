# Release Planning

Release planning is the process of preparing the organization to successfully deliver value to customers.

A release is more than deploying software.

It includes technical readiness, customer communication, operational planning, training, support, and measuring success after deployment.

The goal isn't simply to release software.

The goal is to deliver value with confidence.

---

# The Purpose of Release Planning

Release planning helps answer questions such as:

- Is the work complete?
- Is the product ready?
- Is the organization ready?
- Are customers ready?
- Do we understand the risks?
- How will we measure success?

A successful release is the result of preparation—not luck.

---

# Release Planning Begins Early

Release planning should begin long before code is complete.

It should evolve alongside discovery and delivery.

Waiting until the end of development often creates unnecessary risk.

Planning early allows teams to identify dependencies, customer impacts, and rollout considerations before they become blockers.

---

# Areas to Consider

Successful releases require coordination across multiple disciplines.

## Product

Product should ensure:

- Business objectives remain valid
- Customer value is clearly communicated
- Documentation is complete
- Stakeholders are informed
- Success metrics are defined

---

## Engineering

Engineering should confirm:

- Development is complete
- Testing is complete
- Performance expectations are met
- Monitoring is in place
- Rollback procedures exist
- Deployment plans are understood

---

## Quality Assurance

QA should verify:

- Acceptance criteria are satisfied
- Regression testing is complete
- Critical workflows have been validated
- Known issues are documented

---

## Support

Support teams should understand:

- What's changing
- Known limitations
- Customer impact
- Troubleshooting guidance
- Escalation procedures

Prepared support teams improve customer confidence after release.

---

## Customer Success & Sales

Customer-facing teams should understand:

- New capabilities
- Customer benefits
- Rollout timing
- Documentation
- Training materials

Clear communication helps customers adopt new functionality more successfully.

---

# Types of Releases

Not every release requires the same level of planning.

Examples include:

## Standard Feature Release

Customer-facing enhancements delivered through the normal release process.

---

## Platform Release

Infrastructure or modernization work that may have limited customer-facing functionality but significant operational impact.

---

## Security Release

Focused on reducing risk and maintaining compliance.

Often requires accelerated timelines.

---

## Customer-Specific Release

Funded work or customer-specific enhancements.

May require additional validation, communication, and rollout coordination.

---

## Emergency Release

Production issues requiring immediate attention.

These releases should follow an expedited—but still documented—process.

- Drafting release notes

- Creating executive updates

# Common Mistakes

## Treating Deployment as the Finish Line

Deployment marks the beginning of customer validation.

Continue monitoring adoption, feedback, and operational health after release.

---

## Waiting Too Long to Plan

Late planning creates unnecessary pressure.

Release planning should begin as soon as work enters delivery.

---

## Ignoring Operational Readiness

Successful releases require more than completed code.

Support, documentation, training, and monitoring are equally important.

---

## Measuring Success by Delivery

Shipping software is not the objective.

Customer adoption and business outcomes determine whether the release was successful.

---

# Measuring Release Success

After each release, review:

- Customer adoption
- Production stability
- Support volume
- Performance metrics
- Customer satisfaction
- Business outcomes
- Lessons learned

Every release should improve both the product and the delivery process.

---

# Success Looks Like

Successful release planning creates confidence across the organization.

Success means:

- Engineering deploys with confidence.
- Product clearly communicates customer value.
- Support is prepared.
- Customers understand what's changing.
- Leadership has visibility into risks and outcomes.

The best releases feel uneventful.

Not because they were simple—but because they were well planned.
