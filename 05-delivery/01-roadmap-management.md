# Roadmap Management

A roadmap is a communication tool—not a commitment.

Its purpose is to communicate direction, priorities, and strategic intent while allowing enough flexibility to adapt as new information emerges.

Healthy roadmaps answer three questions:

- Where are we going?
- Why are we going there?
- What outcomes are we trying to achieve?

They should never attempt to predict every feature or every delivery date months in advance.

---

# The Purpose of a Roadmap

A roadmap aligns the organization around a shared direction.

It helps:

- Product communicate strategy
- Engineering understand priorities
- Leadership make investment decisions
- Sales understand future capabilities
- Customers see long-term direction

A roadmap should create alignment, not certainty.

---

# What Belongs on a Roadmap

Roadmaps should communicate outcomes rather than detailed task lists.

Good roadmap items include:

- Strategic initiatives
- Customer problems
- Platform investments
- Major integrations
- Regulatory work
- Infrastructure modernization
- Product themes

Avoid filling a roadmap with:

- User stories
- Bugs
- Technical tasks
- Sprint work
- Individual backlog items

Those belong in delivery planning.

---

# Organize Around Outcomes

Instead of organizing a roadmap by features, organize it around outcomes.

Examples include:

- Improve Pricebook Performance
- Modernize Platform Architecture
- Simplify Customer Onboarding
- Increase API Adoption
- Improve Reporting Reliability

Outcome-based roadmaps help teams remain flexible while keeping everyone focused on the problem being solved.

---

# Roadmaps Are Living Documents

Customer needs change.

Market conditions change.

Technology changes.

Discovery uncovers new information.

Because of this, roadmaps should evolve.

Updating a roadmap is not a failure.

It is evidence that the organization is learning.

---

# Levels of Roadmaps

Different audiences require different levels of detail.

## Executive Roadmap

Focuses on:

- Strategic investments
- Business outcomes
- Major initiatives
- Portfolio health

---

## Product Roadmap

Focuses on:

- Customer value
- Discovery efforts
- Initiative sequencing
- Cross-team dependencies

---

## Delivery Roadmap

Focuses on:

- Active initiatives
- Milestones
- Releases
- Team coordination

Each roadmap serves a different purpose.

Avoid using one roadmap for every audience.

---

# Prioritizing the Roadmap

Every roadmap item competes for limited capacity.

When evaluating priorities, consider:

- Customer value
- Business value
- Strategic alignment
- Engineering effort
- Risk reduction
- Cost of delay
- Technical dependencies

Priority should reflect organizational value—not simply who asked first.

---

# Balancing Different Types of Work

Healthy roadmaps include more than customer features.

A balanced portfolio often contains:

- Customer-facing enhancements
- Platform modernization
- Technical debt reduction
- Security improvements
- Compliance initiatives
- Operational improvements
- Innovation

Long-term product health depends on investing in both customer value and platform health.

---

# Discovery Drives the Roadmap

Ideas should not enter the roadmap simply because someone requested them.

Discovery helps determine:

- Is this a real problem?
- Is it worth solving?
- Is now the right time?
- What outcome are we trying to achieve?

Only after discovery should initiatives be considered for roadmap placement.

---

# AI and Roadmap Management

AI can improve roadmap management by helping Product teams:

- Summarize customer feedback
- Identify recurring themes
- Detect duplicate requests
- Draft initiative summaries
- Generate executive updates
- Analyze roadmap coverage
- Highlight strategic gaps

AI should support prioritization—not replace it.

Roadmap decisions require business judgment.

---

# Common Mistakes

## Treating the Roadmap as a Contract

A roadmap represents the organization's current understanding.

It should evolve as that understanding improves.

---

## Prioritizing the Loudest Stakeholder

Urgency and importance are not the same.

Roadmaps should reflect organizational priorities—not individual influence.

---

## Managing the Roadmap Like a Backlog

Roadmaps communicate strategic direction.

Backlogs manage execution.

Mixing the two creates unnecessary complexity.

---

## Too Much Detail

Executives don't need story-level detail.

Engineering doesn't need a list of every initiative planned over the next two years.

Adjust the level of detail for the audience.

---

## Ignoring Platform Investments

Organizations that only prioritize customer-facing features eventually slow down.

Modernization, reliability, and technical debt should be visible on the roadmap because they create long-term business value.

---

# Roadmap Reviews

Roadmaps should be reviewed regularly.

Questions to ask include:

- Does this still align with our strategy?
- Have customer priorities changed?
- Has discovery changed our understanding?
- Have new risks emerged?
- Are we investing appropriately across customer, technical, and operational work?

Roadmaps should evolve through intentional review—not through reactive changes.

---

# Success Looks Like

An effective roadmap creates clarity without sacrificing flexibility.

Success means:

- Teams understand organizational priorities.
- Leadership understands upcoming investments.
- Engineering can plan effectively.
- Customers see meaningful progress.
- Product adapts as new information becomes available.

The best roadmaps don't predict the future.

They provide a clear direction while leaving room to learn.
