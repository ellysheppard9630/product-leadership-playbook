# Backlog Health

A backlog is more than a list of work.

It represents the Product organization's current understanding of customer problems, business priorities, and future investment.

A healthy backlog enables Engineering to build with confidence while allowing Product to adapt as priorities evolve.

An unhealthy backlog creates confusion, delays decisions, and slows delivery.

---

# What Is Backlog Health?

Backlog health is the ongoing process of ensuring work is:

- Prioritized
- Understood
- Actionable
- Relevant
- Properly sized
- Ready when Engineering needs it

Healthy backlogs reduce uncertainty.

They do not eliminate it.

---

# Characteristics of a Healthy Backlog

A healthy backlog contains work that is:

## Prioritized

The highest-value work appears first.

Teams should not have to guess what is most important.

Priority should reflect:

- Customer value
- Business value
- Strategic alignment
- Technical investment
- Risk

---

## Ready

Items near the top of the backlog should be ready for implementation.

That doesn't mean every detail is complete.

It means Engineering has enough information to begin work confidently.

---

## Well Understood

The team understands:

- The problem
- The desired outcome
- Success metrics
- Major assumptions
- Known risks

The objective is clarity—not perfection.

---

## Continuously Maintained

Backlogs should evolve continuously.

Items that no longer create value should be updated, reprioritized, or removed.

An old backlog eventually becomes technical debt for Product.

---

# Signs of an Unhealthy Backlog

Common symptoms include:

- Hundreds of unreviewed requests
- Duplicate ideas
- Outdated priorities
- Missing business context
- Stories no longer aligned with strategy
- Work that has sat untouched for years
- Large initiatives with no discovery
- Engineering uncertainty

A backlog should create confidence—not confusion.

---

# Backlog Health Reviews

Backlog health should be reviewed on a regular cadence.

The objective is not to refine every story.

The objective is to ensure upcoming work is ready before Engineering needs it.

Typical review topics include:

- Priority changes
- Discovery status
- Duplicate requests
- Stale backlog items
- Engineering questions
- Risks
- Dependencies
- Upcoming roadmap work

Healthy organizations continuously maintain their backlog instead of performing occasional cleanup projects.

---

# Keep the Backlog Lean

A backlog is not an archive.

If an item has remained untouched for an extended period, ask:

- Is this still valuable?
- Has the problem changed?
- Does another initiative solve this?
- Should it be removed?

Removing work is healthy.

Every backlog item carries maintenance cost.

---

# Product Owns the Backlog

Product is responsible for maintaining backlog health.

Engineering contributes through:

- Technical clarification
- Estimates
- Risk identification
- Dependencies
- Feasibility

Backlog ownership remains with Product.

---

# Engineering Shouldn't Wait for Perfect Stories

Well-written stories are valuable.

Perfect stories are unnecessary.

Engineering should have:

- A clear problem statement
- Expected outcome
- Acceptance criteria appropriate to the work
- Enough context to begin implementation

Discovery continues throughout delivery.

---

# AI and Backlog Health

AI can dramatically reduce backlog maintenance effort.

Examples include:

- Detecting duplicate requests
- Grouping similar work
- Identifying stale backlog items
- Drafting user stories
- Suggesting acceptance criteria
- Summarizing engineering discussions
- Identifying missing information
- Flagging inconsistent priorities

AI should improve backlog quality—not replace Product judgment.

---

# Questions for Every Backlog Review

For each upcoming initiative, ask:

- Is this still a priority?
- Has discovery been completed?
- Is the problem clearly understood?
- Does Engineering have enough information?
- Are dependencies identified?
- Can success be measured?
- Is this the best use of capacity?

---

# Measuring Backlog Health

Rather than measuring the size of the backlog, measure its quality.

Useful indicators include:

- Percentage of roadmap initiatives with completed discovery
- Percentage of upcoming work considered "ready"
- Average age of backlog items
- Number of stale initiatives
- Duplicate request rate
- Backlog review cadence
- Engineering clarification requests
- Time from prioritization to implementation

These metrics help identify where Product can improve preparation before development begins.

---

# Success Looks Like

A healthy backlog enables teams to move quickly without sacrificing quality.

Success means:

- Engineering understands upcoming work.
- Priorities are clear.
- Discovery is completed before implementation.
- Outdated work is regularly removed.
- Product spends less time reacting and more time planning.

Backlog health is not measured by the number of stories.

It is measured by the team's confidence in what comes next.
