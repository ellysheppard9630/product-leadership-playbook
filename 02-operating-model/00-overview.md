# Product Operating Model

Every Product organization eventually reaches a point where its processes no longer scale.

More meetings.
More approvals.
More handoffs.
More status updates.

Ironically, these processes are often introduced to improve alignment, but over time they create friction that slows delivery and obscures ownership.

This operating model is my approach to solving that problem.

Rather than adding more process, it focuses on creating clear ownership, reducing unnecessary coordination, and enabling teams to move faster with confidence.

---

# Why This Model Exists

Traditional Product organizations often assign a Product Owner to every engineering team.

While this works well for small organizations, it becomes increasingly difficult to scale.

Common symptoms include:

- Product spending more time managing ceremonies than customers.
- Engineering waiting on Product decisions.
- Duplicate discovery efforts.
- Inconsistent prioritization.
- PMs stretched across too many initiatives.
- Teams optimizing for output instead of outcomes.

This operating model addresses those challenges by redefining ownership and allowing Product and Engineering to focus on their strengths.

---

# Core Principles

This operating model is built around five ideas.

## 1. Product owns business decisions.

Product determines:

- What problems are worth solving.
- Priority.
- Customer outcomes.
- Success metrics.

## 2. Engineering owns technical decisions.

Engineering determines:

- Architecture.
- Implementation.
- Technical design.
- Performance.
- Scalability.
- Maintainability.

## 3. Discovery should match the work.

Not every initiative requires the same level of Product involvement.

Customer-facing initiatives require Product-led discovery.

Technical initiatives often benefit from Engineering-led discovery.

Choosing the appropriate discovery path reduces unnecessary work while maintaining alignment.

## 4. AI should remove administrative work.

AI is used to accelerate documentation, research, analysis, and communication so Product teams can spend more time with customers and stakeholders.

## 5. Product organizations should optimize for outcomes, not activity.

Success isn't measured by:

- Number of meetings.
- Number of stories.
- Velocity.
- Features shipped.

Success is measured by customer and business outcomes.
