# Product vs. Engineering

One of the biggest sources of friction in software organizations isn't process—it's unclear ownership.

When Product and Engineering both believe they own the same decision, progress slows. Meetings multiply, approvals stack up, and accountability becomes blurred.

A healthy operating model doesn't remove collaboration. It creates clarity around who is responsible for making which decisions.

The goal isn't to separate Product and Engineering.

The goal is to create trust through clear ownership.

---

# Guiding Principle

> Product owns **why** and **what**. Engineering owns **how**.

Product defines the customer problem, business value, and desired outcome.

Engineering determines the best technical solution to achieve that outcome.

Both teams collaborate throughout the process, but each has a clear area of accountability.

---

# Product Responsibilities

Product is responsible for maximizing business value and ensuring the team is solving the right problems.

Product owns:

- Product vision
- Strategy
- Roadmap
- Customer discovery
- Market research
- Prioritization
- Business requirements
- User experience direction
- Success metrics
- Stakeholder communication
- Customer validation
- Release readiness

Product should continually ask:

- Are we solving the right problem?
- Is this the highest-value opportunity?
- What outcome are we trying to achieve?
- How will we measure success?

---

# Engineering Responsibilities

Engineering is responsible for delivering reliable, scalable, and maintainable solutions.

Engineering owns:

- Technical architecture
- System design
- APIs
- Database design
- Performance
- Security
- Scalability
- Technical feasibility
- Code quality
- Development practices
- Testing strategy
- Deployment
- Operational health

Engineering should continually ask:

- What's the simplest technical solution?
- Will this scale?
- Is it maintainable?
- What risks exist?
- How can we reduce complexity?

# What Great Collaboration Looks Like

Healthy Product and Engineering organizations don't work in sequence.

They work in partnership.

Product should involve Engineering early during discovery to identify technical risks and opportunities before commitments are made.

Engineering should involve Product when technical decisions create customer-facing tradeoffs.

Both disciplines should challenge assumptions, ask questions, and contribute ideas throughout the lifecycle of an initiative.

Disagreement is healthy.

Unclear ownership is not.

---

# Common Anti-Patterns

## ❌ Product Designing the Solution

Product should describe the customer problem and desired outcome—not prescribe the technical implementation.

Instead of:

> "Build a new microservice with Kafka."

Focus on:

> "Customers need pricing updates to reach stores within five minutes."

---

## ❌ Engineering Prioritizing the Roadmap

Engineering provides technical recommendations and estimates.

Product is responsible for deciding which problems are most valuable to solve.

---

## ❌ Throwing Requirements Over the Wall

Product should not disappear after writing requirements.

Discovery continues throughout delivery.

Likewise, Engineering should not wait until implementation begins to raise concerns.

Continuous collaboration produces better outcomes.

---

## ❌ Optimizing for Output

Shipping more stories does not necessarily create more customer value.

Both Product and Engineering should focus on measurable outcomes rather than the volume of work completed.

---

# Different Types of Work Require Different Levels of Product Involvement

Not every initiative requires the same operating model.

Customer-facing initiatives often require significant Product involvement through discovery, validation, stakeholder communication, and rollout planning.

Technical initiatives such as platform modernization, infrastructure improvements, security work, or architectural enhancements are often best led by Engineering with Product providing business context and prioritization.

Matching the discovery process to the nature of the work allows both organizations to operate more efficiently.

---

# Success Looks Like

When Product and Engineering have clear ownership:

- Decisions happen faster.
- Meetings become shorter and more focused.
- Engineering has greater autonomy.
- Product spends more time with customers.
- Teams experience fewer handoffs.
- Accountability is clear.
- Customer outcomes improve.

The objective isn't to reduce collaboration.

It's to ensure collaboration happens where it creates value instead of where ownership is unclear.
