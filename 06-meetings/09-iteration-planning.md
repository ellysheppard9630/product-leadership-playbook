# Iteration Planning

Iteration Planning is an Engineering-owned planning session where the team commits to the work they believe they can successfully deliver during the upcoming iteration.

Unlike traditional Sprint Planning, Product is not responsible for presenting work or assigning priorities.

Those decisions have already been made through Discovery, Backlog Health Review, Delivery Sync, and Pre-Refinement.

By the time work reaches Iteration Planning, Engineering owns delivery.

The purpose of this meeting is to organize execution.

---

# Purpose

Iteration Planning answers one question:

> **Given our available capacity, what work can we confidently deliver during the next iteration?**

Engineering determines how much work to commit to and how that work will be executed.

---

# Ownership

Iteration Planning is owned by Engineering.

Engineering is responsible for:

- Selecting work from the approved delivery pipeline
- Confirming team capacity
- Establishing the iteration goal
- Organizing implementation
- Assigning ownership where appropriate
- Identifying execution risks

Product does not assign work during this meeting.

---

# Product's Role

Product attendance is optional.

In most cases, Product should not attend.

Engineering should only invite Product when:

- A business clarification is required
- Priorities have changed unexpectedly
- Discovery uncovered new information
- Engineering needs additional context

If Product regularly attends Iteration Planning, it may indicate earlier meetings are not providing enough clarity.

---

# Objectives

During each Iteration Planning session, Engineering should:

- Review available capacity
- Confirm the iteration goal
- Select work from the approved backlog
- Validate dependencies
- Review technical risks
- Organize implementation
- Confirm team commitment

The objective is realistic planning—not maximizing utilization.

---

# Typical Attendees

Required

- Engineering Team
- Engineering Manager

Recommended

- Technical Lead
- QA

Optional

- Architect
- Product (only when requested)

---

# Recommended Cadence

At the beginning of each sprint or planned iteration.

Kanban teams may instead conduct lightweight planning whenever capacity becomes available.

---

# Typical Agenda

## Review Capacity

Discuss:

- Planned time off
- On-call responsibilities
- Support commitments
- Training
- Team availability

Capacity should reflect reality rather than ideal conditions.

---

## Review Approved Work

Engineering reviews initiatives that have already completed:

- Discovery
- Pre-Refinement
- Engineering Refinement

The work entering Iteration Planning should already be:

- Prioritized
- Understood
- Estimated
- Ready for implementation

This meeting is not used to debate priorities.

---

## Define the Iteration Goal

Engineering establishes a clear objective for the iteration.

Examples include:

- Complete customer onboarding improvements
- Deliver API enhancements
- Modernize authentication service
- Improve reporting performance

The goal should describe the value the team intends to deliver.

---

## Commit to Work

Engineering selects the amount of work it believes it can successfully complete.

Commitments should balance:

- Capacity
- Complexity
- Risk
- Dependencies
- Operational work

The goal is predictability—not maximizing workload.

---

## Review Risks

Identify:

- Cross-team dependencies
- Infrastructure work
- Technical uncertainty
- External blockers
- Production support commitments

Addressing risks early improves delivery confidence.

---

# Inputs

Typical inputs include:

- Approved backlog
- Engineering estimates
- Capacity forecast
- Technical dependencies
- Engineering Refinement outputs

---

# Outputs

Each Iteration Planning session should produce:

- Iteration goal
- Team commitment
- Selected work
- Assigned ownership
- Updated delivery forecast
- Identified execution risks

Engineering should leave with confidence in its delivery plan.

---

# AI Can Help

AI can assist Engineering by:

- Summarizing iteration scope
- Highlighting capacity risks
- Identifying dependency conflicts
- Suggesting implementation sequencing
- Creating engineering task lists
- Drafting iteration summaries

AI supports planning while leaving commitment decisions to the team.

---

# Common Mistakes

## Reprioritizing Work

Priorities should already be established before Iteration Planning begins.

If priorities need to change, return to the Product delivery process rather than changing them during Engineering planning.

---

## Overcommitting

Teams consistently build trust by delivering predictable outcomes.

Avoid committing to more work than the team can realistically complete.

---

## Treating Planning as a Product Meeting

Iteration Planning is an Engineering planning session.

Engineering owns execution and should determine how work is organized.

---

## Ignoring Operational Work

Production support, maintenance, technical debt, and unexpected work consume capacity.

Successful teams plan for reality rather than ideal conditions.

---

# Success Measures

Iteration Planning is successful when:

- Engineering commits to realistic goals.
- Capacity is understood.
- Risks are identified early.
- Work is clearly organized.
- Development begins without unnecessary uncertainty.

The meeting succeeds when Engineering can focus on delivery instead of continually adjusting commitments throughout the iteration.

---

# Relationship to Other Meetings

| Engineering Refinement | Iteration Planning |
|------------------------|--------------------|
| Plan how work will be implemented | Commit work for the iteration |
| Estimate effort | Confirm capacity |
| Break down implementation | Organize execution |
| Technical discussion | Team commitment |

| Iteration Planning | Project Checkpoint |
|--------------------|--------------------|
| Plan upcoming work | Review work in progress |
| Engineering-only | Product & Engineering |
| Commit to delivery | Validate business alignment |

Iteration Planning marks the point where Engineering fully accepts ownership of delivery.

Product has defined **what** and **why**.

Engineering now determines **when** and **how** the work will be delivered.
